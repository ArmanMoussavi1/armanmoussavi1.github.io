Research

My work bridges molecular simulation, machine learning, and materials theory to understand and design the next generation of soft and nanoscale materials — from polymer-grafted nanoparticles to hydrogels — with real-world implications in sustainable separations and structural resilience.

Graduate Research — Keten Laboratory, Northwestern University

Advisor: Prof. Sinan Keten • Sept 2022 – Present

Polymer-Grafted Nanoparticle Mechanics

Polymer-grafted nanoparticles (PGNs) are single-component materials with rich, tunable mechanical behavior driven by molecular architecture. My work develops theoretical frameworks and molecular dynamics simulations using LAMMPS to understand how grafting density, chain length, and core size govern shear response, toughness, and assembly behavior.

A key discovery: nanoconfinement suppresses chain interdigitation and entanglement during assembly. Releasing that confinement allows chains to re-entangle, dramatically toughening the resulting solid — with implications for structural adhesives and impact-resistant coatings.

PGN Gas Separation Membranes

In collaboration with Prof. Sanat Kumar (Columbia University), I investigate the gas transport properties of PGN membranes — a promising alternative to traditional polymer membranes for industrial separations (e.g., CO₂ capture, H₂ purification).

We developed a two-channel permeability model that separates contributions from the polymer brush matrix and nanoparticle-mediated free volume, providing a predictive framework to overcome the Robeson upper-bound tradeoff between permeability and selectivity. This work was published in Macromolecules (2025).

Hydrogel Fracture & Toughening

In collaboration with Prof. Jian-Ping Kim (Northwestern MechE), I study fracture toughness of hydrogels — soft, water-swollen networks with applications in biomedical devices, soft robotics, and protective materials.

Current work focuses on viscous dissipation and monomeric friction as toughening mechanisms, and on using network topology (graph-theoretic measures) to predict and design fracture resistance. A companion project develops deep-learning-based coarse-grained models capable of reproducing hydrogel mechanics at larger scales.

2020 – 2022

Bio-Inspired Roadside Safety Hardware

Advised by Prof. Cody Jin, I investigated how biological structures (e.g., trabecular bone, plant stems, insect exoskeletons) can inspire crashworthy roadside safety barriers and guardrails. The project resulted in a review article published in Materials & Design (2025) documenting the state of the art and identifying avenues for bio-inspired structural design.

Post-Disaster Digital Reconnaissance

Advised by Prof. Christine Wittich, I developed methods for assessing structural damage after natural hazards using digital sources — satellite imagery, social media, and local news articles. This work contributed to the emerging field of virtual (remote) reconnaissance, presented at the SNERP Symposium (2020) and published in the Forensic Engineering 2022 conference proceedings.
